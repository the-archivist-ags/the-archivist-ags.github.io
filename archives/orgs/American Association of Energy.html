SOURCES - A Publishment by the American Association of Energy
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Issue 1 - Hydropower - Reliable & Viable. --- Reliability Hydropower is overall a very reliable source, compared to other renewable sources, and even over fossil fuels such as coal due to some conditional factors. Hydropower facilities can quickly change from between zero output to maximum output, making them exceptionally good at meeting changing demands for electricity throughout the day. Besides being more efficient, hydropower runs continuously and consistently with little maintenance needed. If needed, it can actually store power: pumped storage, a type of hydropower technology, accumulates the electricity generated by other power sources like solar, wind, and nuclear for later use by storing energy in the form of water in an upper reservoir, pumped from a second reservoir at a lower elevation. --- Potential and Overall Viability Hydropower is known as one of the most efficient ways to generate electricity, with it converting 90% of total energy into electricity, whereas fossil fuels often top around 50%. It is extremely viable, seeing the immense water infrastructure around the country, its reliability, and the techniques previously mentioned, such as pumped storage which can store energy. Moreover, the USA has a large potential of nonpowered dams and conduits, which if equipped with turbines, can largely expand reliable renewable energy. A study found around 54000 nonpowered dams in the United States which can be retrofitted to generate power. This would make our energy production and utilization of resources much more efficient. In short, Hydropower has an immense potential in the United States, to be expanded, seeing the development of various technologies which make it more viable across the nation, especially seeing the cheapness of production, which we will expand on in our next issue.

SOURCES - A Publishment by the American Association of Energy
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Issue 2 - Hydropower - Economics, Efforts. --- Economics/Costs Hydropower is an unexpensive source for electricity, only costing around 0.85 cents to produce per kilo-watt hour. This is about half as much of nuclear, and about a fourth of the cost of natural gas in turbines, as there is no fuel cost. This is another nod to the environmental benefits, as well as economic, with the only fuel being the water running through, making this source fully ''green'', or should we say blue, in that aspect. Moreover, hydropower does not experience rising or unstable fuel costs. From 1985 to 1990 the cost of operating a hydropower plant grew at less than the rate of inflation. This besides being positive economically, alludes to its longterm reliability, it being a self-dependent source, not requiring any imports/exports, or uncertain weather conditions. --- Efforts and Government Actions Only around 3% of the nation's 80,000 existing dams are used to generate power. Installing turbines in existing dams would expand this powerful and cost-efficient power source. However, from 2005 to 2015 the DoE (department of energy) had spent around 1,200,000,000 dollars on research and development for other renewable sources like wind, solar, and geothermal, but only around 10 million dollars on hydropower. This is truly saddening, seeing how hydropower is much more viable and beneficial in many areas. Currently, there hasn't been much actions for this source, but there has been a lot done for nuclear energy, which we aim to address shortly. Senator Hagen has told us that he will be introducing a bill on this soon however, and from our interviews with Order and Phoenix, it seems as if this source is supported by most of our elected politicians.(edited)

---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Issue 3 - Hydropower - Environment, Regulation, Misc. --- Environmental Effects Hydropower leaves behind no waste. The only non-continuous sources used are small ones for maintenance, and manufacturing of the turbine system. It produces no green house gases, nor any air pollution at all. Furthermore, according to some estimates, it prevents the burning of 22 billion gallons of oil, or 120 million tonnes of coal every year! It has no fuel, except if you count water, which goes through a continuous cycle without any pollution left behind. --- Regulation Federal regulations harm the growth of hydropower and the reliable renewable sector. The FERC stifles hydropower development through a complex, unpredictable, and costly licensing process. We would encourage government officials who work towards implementing an efficient licensing system for the benefit of our nation. Other Hydropower reservoirs strongly boost local economies, and the lives of people. A study of one medium-sized hydropower project in Wisconsin showed that the recreational value to residents and visitors exceeded $6.5 million annually. This can be understood in how Reservoirs formed by hydroelectric dams provide many water-based recreational opportunities including fishing, water sports, boating, and water fowl hunting, hydro operators use land around reservoirs for public use, for hunting, hiking, and snow activities such as snowmobiling or skiing, with also other activities like boating, public beaches, picnic areas, fishing, canoe dockings, etc.

AAOE - Current Politics #1
Review on the Fossil Fuel Efficiency Bill of 2020 This bill firstly creates a commission to head the transitioning of fossil fuels to nuclear energy by scouting locations for expanding and creating nuclear plants. We believe this is a good step forward to make, by specifically aligning the commission to this one agenda under the supervision of the Department of Energy. Moreover, it accounts for the coronavirus problem, saying the commission will be enacted 8 months after the bill is passed, allowing for the pandemic to decrease in size before any such activity is done. Moreover, it gives a ''5% tax discount'' if 8% of a companys sales constitute from Nuclear Energy up to a certain date. We feel putting a limit on this till 2035 is unnecessary, as nuclear energy takes time to develop, and this should be continuous. However, we feel this isn't too significant of a factor here. It also sets up an EPA managed program to train fossil fuel industry workers for the renewable and green energy sector jobs, which we believe is essential, to help the employment rates and the people of the country. Furthermore, the bill distributes 66 million dollars between the states for developing green energy oriented trade schools, which can be essential in luring people towards those jobs, and giving them specific and accessible training in the sector. It also bans leases for new public lands after 2030, while allowing existing ones. Overall, we support this bill in its entirety.

AAOE - Current Politics #3
Review on the Hydropower Expansion Act of 2020 This bill seems to be a more comprehensive and viable version of the bill put forward in the House, while also expanding in some other essential areas. First of all, it starts with a section called identification. This addresses the DoE's involvement on multiple fronts to scout areas for the purpose of hydropower expansion, such as a list of public non-power dams, and to scout private areas for the same reason. It also connects the Army Corps here. We believe this is a good step towards identifying the expansions to be made. Secondly, it gives funding to the DoE to work with the Army Corps and other necessary institutions to equip publicly owned dams with hydropower systems, and also to start a flexible loan system for private companies to develop their own with ''minimal interest''. We believe this is a necessary step to encourage hydropower development, and also increase its capibilities. Then it also makes multiple administrative changes, related to the licensing. It strips the FERC of licensing ability, and gives it to the DoE, probably citing discouraging licensing factors set by the FERC, which have inabled the expansion of Hydropower. It also said that the DoE must award licenses at a reasonable cost. Overall, we support the bill.
